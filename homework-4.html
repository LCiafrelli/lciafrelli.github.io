Homework 4: Law of Large Numbers Simulation

Interactive demonstration of how empirical frequencies converge to theoretical probability as sample size increases.

Part A — Theory: Law of Large Numbers

1 Concept & Mathematical Statement

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) is a fundamental theorem in probability theory stating that as the number of trials increases, the empirical average converges to the expected value.

Intuition: Whether flipping a fair coin or rolling a die, as you repeat the experiment many times, the observed proportion approaches the theoretical probability.

Formal Statement:
Let X₁, X₂, ..., Xₙ be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables with expected value E[X] = μ
lim (n→∞) [ (X₁ + X₂ + ... + Xₙ) / n ] = μ
For relative frequency f(n):
f(n) = (successes) / n → p as n → ∞
2 Weak vs Strong Law

Weak Law (WLLN): Convergence in probability

P( |f(n) - p| > ε ) → 0 as n → ∞

Strong Law (SLLN): Almost sure convergence

P( lim(n→∞) f(n) = p ) = 1
3 Variance Reduction & Convergence

Variance decreases with n:

Var(f(n)) = p(1-p) / n

For Fair Coin (p=0.5):

  • Variance: 0.25/n
  • Standard Error: 0.5/√n
  • Maximum variance among all probabilities

For Fair Die (p=1/6 ≈ 0.167):

  • Variance: (1/6)(5/6)/n ≈ 0.139/n
  • Standard Error: √(5/36)/√n ≈ 0.373/√n
  • Lower variance than coin, faster convergence
4 Why These Experiments?

Fair Coin (p=0.5):

  • Maximum variance: p(1-p) peaks at 0.25 when p=0.5
  • Represents the "worst case" for convergence speed
  • Symmetric and intuitive
  • Classic probability example

Fair Die Rolling 6 (p=1/6):

  • Lower probability event (p≈0.167 vs 0.5)
  • Shows LLN works for asymmetric cases
  • Lower variance → potentially faster convergence
  • More discrete outcomes to observe

Educational Value: Comparing both experiments shows that LLN is universal—it works regardless of the specific probability value.
