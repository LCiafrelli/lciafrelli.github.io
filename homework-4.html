Homework 4: Law of Large Numbers Simulation

Interactive demonstration of how empirical frequencies converge to theoretical probability as sample size increases.

Part A — Theory: Law of Large Numbers

1 Concept & Mathematical Statement

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) is a fundamental theorem in probability theory stating that as the number of trials increases, the empirical average converges to the expected value.

Intuition: If you flip a fair coin many times, the proportion of heads will get closer and closer to 0.5 as the number of flips increases.

Formal Statement:
Let X₁, X₂, ..., Xₙ be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables with expected value E[X] = μ
lim (n→∞) [ (X₁ + X₂ + ... + Xₙ) / n ] = μ
Or equivalently, for relative frequency f(n) of successes:
f(n) = (number of successes) / n → p as n → ∞
Where p is the true probability of success
2 Weak vs Strong Law

Weak Law of Large Numbers (WLLN):

States that f(n) converges to p in probability. For any ε > 0:

P( |f(n) - p| > ε ) → 0 as n → ∞

This means the probability that f(n) deviates from p by more than ε becomes arbitrarily small.

Strong Law of Large Numbers (SLLN):

States that f(n) converges to p almost surely (with probability 1):

P( lim(n→∞) f(n) = p ) = 1

This is a stronger statement: almost every sequence of trials will converge to p.

3 Convergence Properties

Variance Reduction: As n increases, the variance of f(n) decreases:

Var(f(n)) = p(1-p) / n

For a fair coin (p=0.5): Var(f(n)) = 0.25 / n

  • n = 10: Var = 0.025 (high variability)
  • n = 100: Var = 0.0025 (moderate variability)
  • n = 1000: Var = 0.00025 (low variability)

Standard Error: The standard deviation of f(n):

SE(f(n)) = √[p(1-p)/n] ≈ 0.5/√n (for p=0.5)

This shows error decreases proportionally to 1/√n, not 1/n.

4 Why Use p = 0.5 and Fair Coin Flips?

Choice of p = 0.5 (Fair Coin):

  • Maximum variance: p(1-p) is maximized at p=0.5, giving variance of 0.25. This represents the "hardest" case for convergence.
  • Symmetry: Results are symmetric around 0.5, making visual interpretation clearer.
  • Intuitive: Everyone understands a fair coin flip - it's the canonical example in probability.
  • Educational value: Shows convergence even in the worst-case scenario for variance.

Interpretation: If the LLN works well for p=0.5 (maximum uncertainty), it will work even better for other values of p.
