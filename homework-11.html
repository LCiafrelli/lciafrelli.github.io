What is Brownian Motion?

Brownian motion describes the random movement of objects undergoing constant, unpredictable perturbations. The key insight: motion is continuous, not jumpy‚Äîyou can follow a path over time with no sudden discontinuities. At any small time interval, the object moves by a small random amount, independent of past movements.

From Discrete to Continuous

In Homework 7, we modeled discrete random walks: at each tick, step left or right. The Wiener process is the continuous analog‚Äîimagine letting time steps shrink to zero while keeping total variability constant.

$$\text{Discrete:} \quad S_n = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n$$ $$\text{Continuous:} \quad W(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \text{(scaled sum)}$$

Defining Characteristics

  • Zero start: \(W(0) = 0\) always
  • Independent increments: Non-overlapping intervals are independent
  • Gaussian increments: \(\Delta W \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Delta t)\) (normal with variance = time step)
  • Continuous paths: No jumps, only smooth variations

Simulation via Discretization

Divide [0,T] into \(n\) steps of size \(\Delta t = T/n\). At each step, add a small normal increment:

$$W_{i+1} = W_i + \sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot Z_i$$ $$\text{where } Z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

This builds up the path incrementally. The \(\sqrt{\Delta t}\) scaling ensures the variance matches theory.

Arithmetic Brownian Motion (ABM)

Add a trend (drift) and amplify randomness (volatility):

$$X(t) = \mu t + \sigma W(t)$$

The term \(\mu t\) creates systematic drift; \(\sigma W(t)\) adds noise around this trend. This models real processes like stock prices or temperature changes.

Box-Muller: Uniform ‚Üí Normal

Computers generate uniforms easily. To get normals, use this elegant transform:

$$Z = \sqrt{-2\ln(U_1)} \cos(2\pi U_2) \quad \sim \quad \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

where \(U_1, U_2 \sim U(0,1)\). This is what powers the simulation‚Äîtwo uniforms give one standard normal.

Convergence to Theory

As \(n \to \infty\) (more steps, finer grid), the discrete sum converges to the true continuous Wiener process. The empirical distribution of final values becomes increasingly normal, matching theoretical predictions‚Äîthis is the Functional Central Limit Theorem.
